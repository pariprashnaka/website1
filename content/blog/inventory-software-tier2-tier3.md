Walk into most inventory software comparisons and you'll find the same handful of names, all built around the same assumption: a single business type, a single location, and a stable internet connection. That assumption holds fine for a retail chain in a metro city. It breaks down fast for a distributor running mixed product lines out of a tier-2 or tier-3 town.

## The gap isn't features — it's fit

Looking across the inventory and ERP tools currently serving Indian small businesses, the market splits into two camps. On one side, enterprise ERP systems with far more configuration than a small operation needs, priced and structured for companies with a dedicated IT function. On the other, single-vertical tools — built specifically for one kind of retail, one kind of stock, one kind of workflow — that don't flex when a business sells across categories or operates with informal credit relationships (a *khata* system, in practical terms) that most software doesn't model at all.

The businesses caught in between are exactly the ones with the least patience for either extreme. A distributor moving multiple product categories through a network of retail relationships doesn't need enterprise ERP complexity, and doesn't fit neatly into a single-category retail tool either.

## What actually matters at this scale

A few requirements come up consistently once you look closely at how these businesses actually operate, rather than how software vendors assume they operate:

- **GST-native from the start, not bolted on.** Compliance isn't optional, and treating it as an add-on module usually means it's the first thing that breaks under real use.
- **Offline-capable.** Connectivity in smaller towns isn't always reliable enough to assume a constant connection, and software that simply stops working without signal isn't usable in daily operations.
- **Configurable per vertical, without a developer.** A business selling steel utensils and one selling agricultural inputs have different units, different seasonal patterns, and different customer relationships — but shouldn't need two different pieces of software, or a custom build, to get software that fits either one.
- **Priced for the actual transaction volume.** Enterprise ERP pricing models often don't map cleanly onto a business doing high-frequency, lower-margin transactions.

## The honest tradeoff

None of this means every small distributor needs custom software. Plenty are genuinely well served by an off-the-shelf tool, particularly if their operation is single-vertical and their connectivity is reliable. The distinction worth making before choosing anything is whether your business fits the assumptions the software was actually built around — not just whether it has the features on the list.

If it doesn't, the cost isn't a dramatic failure on day one. It's a slow accumulation of workarounds — a spreadsheet here, a WhatsApp message there — until the "system" is actually three systems held together by habit, and nobody trusts any of them completely.
